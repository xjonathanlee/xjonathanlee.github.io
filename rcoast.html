Raincoast Conservation

Launched in 1979 Raincoast conservation is a team of scientists and conservationists that strive to safeguard British Columbias most precious ecological resources. I was brought on as a data storyteller to help craft a story that could get more people interested in the challenges of Early spring salmon in the Fraser River.

  • Roles:Visual Design, Art Direction, Archival Research, Research Creation
  • Tools: Adobe Illustrator, Adobe Photoshop, Observable, Excel, Javascript, HTML + CSS,Svelte
  • Year: 2023
  • Context: Client Internship (7 weeks)
  • Deliverables: Data Visualization , Scrolly-telling website

Problem

How can we tell the 40 year decline and collaspe of Fraser River Chinook?

Design Process

Beginning with the data set, we were keen on exploring a visually expressive way to tell information to lay-persons passionate about British Columbias unique aquatic ecosystems. Given Salmon is an economic driver and a unique resource of BC 86% of lay people are concerned about the declining salmon stocks in BC, with a further 48% are concerned about hatcheries and farmed salmon (PSF,2022).

After discussion my team knew we needed an expressive way to tell the story of 40 years of decline without sounding cliche, looking at historical precedence and touch-stones that could ground an experience in nostalgia and fact.

Final Presentation can be found here.

Designing for the User

I proposed a two fold solution that allowed the data to tell an emerging story, coupled with a narrative that could help illustrate the strong historical ties to the salmon trade the Albion Fisheries had.

  1. A microsite that used "scrolly-telling".
  2. A data visual that could explain decline accurately.

During our research we uncovered salmon conservation stories and efforts from the government using structured data from the Albion Test Fisheries. Traditionally the data formatted was compiled by PDF by week, making extraction of the data messy and hard to parse across a large time frame. Fortunately, we were granted access to the DFO database server to create a tidy CSV.

Visual Impact

The visualization and narrative content helps Raincoast stand out in a crowd of papers and figures by crafting a unique data driven graphic and narrative story telling experience that is aligned with scientific rigour expected from a conservation group.

The result is a narrative piece and a data driven visualization that uses empathy and history to help connect research and narratives visually. Resulting in an lower barrier to entry and increasing access to knowledge.

Interaction

By allowing users to pace themselves, one can look at the content as a way to inform users without overwhelming them. Given a high level overhead combined with a reflection section that drives home intent across different levels of media (mixing article and scroll interactions), we can ensure users adopt and process the message we are telling them instead of mindlessly scrolling.

Putting it all Together

The initial implementation ran into many problems... cheifly among them being an extremely slow loading speed (at 36.6 seconds!). With this in mind (and 4 monthes after my internship ended) I wanted to expand on this project and give it the pizazz that it truly deserved. Peeking under the hood, I noticed that the video was among the things causing a loading problem, even at a reduced downsampled rate the loading times were atrocious.

I wanted to restructure the website to allow for a better flow, but more importantly I wanted to reduce the lag time between sections, and maybe in the background add a few more interactive components that could enhance the reader's experience. The current iteration although sleek on my computer, failed to meet my recently honed knowledge of good / clean UX and content strategy.

Looking at how other articles were structured I realized mine relied on the video to do heavy lifting, to reduce this I came up with a different type of plan in order to mitigate these loading problems. Breaking up the video into chunks would allow more efficient loading, but also allocating some resources to interactive elements would also help guide users. In order to do this though I would need to access the original unpruned version of the script, or barring that create a new script that would be a suitable article length.
