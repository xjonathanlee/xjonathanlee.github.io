The Fraser River ecosystem in Canada stands as one of North America's vital salmon habitats that provide areas of refuge for Salmon. These watersheds; defined by the collection and flow of rainfall and snowmelt, direct freshwater into streams, rivers, lakes, and eventually the ocean.

Keystone species, supported by the varied freshwater ecosystems present along the Fraser and its estuaries, play a pivotal role in shaping ecosystems from the bottom up. Chinook Salmon as keystone species are held sacred by the First Nations Communities that live amongst the Fraser River and its Tributaries. These Salmon, especially the Chinook are integral in shaping the landscape of the Fraser River region, providing sustenance to wildlife, people, and the land itself(). The Chinook Salmon, often referred to as 'spring' salmon in Canada and 'king' salmon in the United States due to their early return and large size, are facing a decline in returning stocks. Chinook salmon exhibit significant diversity within their species, with distinctions between 'stream types' and 'ocean types.'

Stream-type Chinook, spend a full year in the river ecosystems before migrating to the ocean, spawning in the spring and early summer. These river-staying fish are special to the Chinook populations among the Fraser, in addition to being larger and stronger than ocean-type chinook, they have higher fat reserves when returning, making them a critical species for animals looking to up their calorie intake for the brutal winter months ahead, they are often the lifeline for bears approaching hibernation season as their huge fat reserves are a much needed caloric boost.
